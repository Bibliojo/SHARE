This disparate situation leaves sole dependence on petroleum as a transportation fuel unattractive for many countries, including the United States. As the United States looks to diversify its portfolio&#xd;

Ethanol and biodiesel have been the dominant fuels to-date, but new production methods and alternative feedstocks may bolster their suitability for wide-scale adoption. <dc:description>This study investigates whether the information regarding the safety of genetically modified (GM) corn and GM corn oil can significantly affect the Japanese consumers' risk perceptions and whether these risk perceptions can determine their willingness-to-pay (WTP) premium for non-GM corn oil. This WTP premium was measured by using a contingent valuation method that is commonly applied for valuing non-market commodities.&#xd;
Our empirical results show that, when the scientific knowledge of the safety of GM corn and GM corn oil was provided, the risk perceptions expressed by the mean of a probability distribution and those by the variance both substantially decreased, while the WTP unchanged with the given information. In addition, Japanese&#xd;
consumers' premium percentage for non-GM corn oil was found to be about 40% of the price of the cooking corn oil sold in markets.</dc:description>

<dc:description>This paper provides evidence on organizational structure, geographic diversification, and performance at bank holding companies (BHCs). First, we show that a BHC's member banks benefit from access to the parent organization's internal capital market. Second, we ask if the benefits of internal capital markets are best realized within loosely structured, decentralized organizations or more consolidated, centralized firms. We find that BHCs with many subsidiaries are less profitable and have lower q ratios than similar BHCs with fewer subsidiaries. However, because we study multi-unit firms in a single industry, our results suggest that the valuation discount reported in the diversification literature in empirical corporate finance reflects not only&#xd;
industry diversification, but also organizational structure.</dc:description>

<dc:title>Opportunity discovery, entrepreneurial action, and economic organization</dc:title>
<dc:creator>Klein, Peter G.</dc:creator>
<dc:subject>Entrepreneurship</dc:subject>
<dc:description>"Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal, forthcoming"</dc:description>
<dc:description>"This version: 5 June 2008"</dc:description>
<dc:description>This paper reviews and critiques the "opportunity discovery"   approach to entrepreneurship and argues that entrepreneurship can be more thoroughly grounded, and more closely linked to more general problems of economic organization, by adopting the Cantillon-Knight-Mises understanding of entrepreneurship as judgment. I begin by distinguishing among occupational, structural, and functional approaches to entrepreneurship and distinguishing among two&#xd;
influential interpretations of the entrepreneurial function, discovery and judgment. I turn next to the contemporary literature on opportunity identification and argue that this literature misinterprets Kirzner's instrumental use of the discovery metaphor and mistakenly makes "opportunities" the unit of analysis. I then describe an alternative approach in which investment is the unit of analysis and link this approach to Austrian capital theory. I close with some applications to organizational form and entrepreneurial teams.</dc:description>

remaining resistance to this technology. The political&#xd;
response to the on-going development of Golden Rice is&#xd;

<dc:description>We assess 47 peer-reviewed articles that have applied stated&#xd;
economics methods to measure the farm-level impacts of Bt&#xd;
cotton in developing agriculture from 1996. We focus on methods,&#xd;
although findings are also contrasted and compared in&#xd;
qualitative terms. The central research question assessed by&#xd;
the articles reviewed is: what are the current and potential&#xd;
advantages of transgenic cotton with respect to yield, pesticide&#xd;
use, input cost, revenue and/or profits at the farm-level, by farm&#xd;
type, and geographical region? We find that, while the evidence&#xd;
is promising, the balance sheet remains inconclusive in part&#xd;
because of some methodological limitations and in part because&#xd;
institutional and political context, which is mutable and often&#xd;
ignored, shapes economic impacts, especially over the longerterm.&#xd;
Most often, the contextual factors that influence whether a&#xd;
new variety succeeds or fails are more critical than whether&#xd;
yield advantages can be demonstrated in on-farm trials.</dc:description>

<dc:description>Many studies report that Bt cotton has led to significant yield&#xd;

region in isolation from adoption in others. This article summarizes&#xd;
the global impacts of Bt cotton adoption in the United&#xd;
States and China based on results from a three-region model of&#xd;
the world cotton market. In 2001, adoption of Bt cotton in China&#xd;
and the United States increased world cotton production by&#xd;
0.7% and reduced the world cotton price by 1.4 cents per&#xd;
pound. Global economic benefits were $836 million. Consumer&#xd;
surplus increased $63 million. Chinese producers gained by&#xd;
$428 million and US producers by $179 million. The fall in world&#xd;
price reduced rest-of-world (ROW) producer surplus by $349&#xd;
million. Net rest-of-world benefits were $69 million, however,&#xd;
because purchaser gains outweighed producer losses.</dc:description>

<dc:description>Whereas most research on public perceptions of genetically&#xd;
modified products have focused on first-generation biotechnologies&#xd;
and genetically modified foods, this paper examines public&#xd;
support for a variety of animal and plant agricultural biotechnology&#xd;
applications and explores whether the determinants of support&#xd;
for each application vary by knowledge, trust, benefits, and&#xd;
sociodemographic variables. The data for this study were gathered&#xd;
from 432 adults in a regional Southwestern telephone survey&#xd;
conducted from March 28 through May 4, 2004. The results&#xd;
revealed that the vehicle used (animal or plant) appears to outweigh&#xd;
both the function and type of application, although nonfood&#xd;
applications tended to receive higher support levels than&#xd;
genetically modified foods. Plant applications received higher&#xd;
support than animal applications. Additionally, the determinants&#xd;
for each biotechnology application were different, and their&#xd;
explanatory power varied by application. Only perceived benefits&#xd;
was significantly related to each biotechnology application.</dc:description>

<dc:description>Cassava is regarded as the crop of last resort for millions of&#xd;

crop by bundling the scarce resources available and uniting the&#xd;
major stakeholders involved in cassava breeding, production,&#xd;
marketing, and consumption worldwide. Based on a small&#xd;
expert survey, this article investigates the importance of the&#xd;
problems in cassava agriculture and the approaches considered&#xd;
to be most effective in addressing them. The author concludes&#xd;
that the CBN represents an innovative, demand-oriented and&#xd;
multistakeholder-driven crop research network that is able to&#xd;
effectively address the challenges in cassava agriculture that&#xd;
were perceived to be most important in the survey. Its innovations&#xd;
could be of particular benefit to Central Africa—a region&#xd;
that is highly vulnerable to starvation and malnutrition and relies&#xd;
mostly on cassava as the main staple food. However, in order to&#xd;
make it happen, some major institutional bottlenecks need to be&#xd;
addressed in the international agricultural research system.</dc:description>

<dc:description>White maize is the staple food of the majority of the South African&#xd;
population. We examine if smallholder farmers that adopted&#xd;
insect-resistant (Bt) varieties of white maize benefited from&#xd;
planting Bt over the last three seasons. Commercial farmers are&#xd;
known to benefit from planting Bt maize in high stalk borer or&#xd;
corn borer infestation years, but when planted in locations or&#xd;
years when stalk borers are not a problem, Bt will usually not be&#xd;
profitable because of higher seed costs. In the first two production&#xd;
seasons, small farmers enjoyed higher yields with Bt maize&#xd;
than with conventional hybrid varieties despite lower-than-normal&#xd;
(yet still significant) stalk borer pressure and less-than-ideal&#xd;
maize production conditions. Yield is expressed according to&#xd;
grain per kilogram of seed planted, as seeding rates per land&#xd;
area differ drastically between small-scale farmers. The value of&#xd;
the yield benefit depended on how the farmer utilized the additional&#xd;
grain, with the highest valued use being home grinding&#xd;
and consumption substituting for more expensive store purchases.&#xd;
In the third season, which was also the fourth consecutive&#xd;
drier-than-usual season, the stalk borer infestation level was&#xd;
very low, and farmers who planted Bt maize had yields similar to&#xd;
farmers who planted conventional hybrids.</dc:description>

basic research, human capital/knowledge (as measured through&#xd;

research creates a “crowding-in” effect for private firms. This&#xd;
model also shows that enhanced intellectual property rights&#xd;
have increased the revenues of private firms.</dc:description>

<dc:description>This paper examines the international patent behavior of nine&#xd;
major firms for seven patent authorities: Australia, Brazil, Canada,&#xd;
China, the European Patent Office (EPO), Japan, and&#xd;
South Africa. The patent sample is based on firms having an initial&#xd;
US patent application; this provides an indication of the&#xd;
amount of technology transfer that occurs from the United&#xd;
States to other countries. Using patent data, the research examines&#xd;
the correlation of firms' patent application decisions based&#xd;
on crop and invention types, the differences in the patent grant&#xd;
rates among the patent authorities, and firms' decisions to pursue&#xd;
patent renewals. The analysis uses empirical evidence to&#xd;
justify possible reasons for the lack of observing much technology&#xd;
transfer from the United States to other countries. Australia,&#xd;
Canada, and the EPO are most likely to receive patent applications.&#xd;
Corn and soybean and gene and method inventions are&#xd;
most likely to be applied for abroad. Approval rates are generally&#xd;
low and vary among patent offices.</dc:description>

<dc:description>Genetically modified (GM) cotton was approved for commercial&#xd;
cultivation in 2002. Hybrids to date have carried the Bt (Bacillus&#xd;
thuringiensis) gene, which confers resistance to Lepidoptera&#xd;
and certain Coleoptera. As well as “official” Bt hybrids (i.e.,&#xd;
those that have gone through a formal approval process), there&#xd;
are “unofficial” Bt hybrids produced without such approval. The&#xd;
owners of the official hybrids, Monsanto-Mahyco, claim that the&#xd;
unofficial hybrids are not as good and could even damage the&#xd;
perception of Bt cotton amongst farmers. Anti-GM groups claim&#xd;
that neither type of Bt hybrid provides either yield or economic&#xd;
advantages over non-Bt hybrids. This paper reports the first&#xd;
study of official versus unofficial versus non-Bt hybrids in India&#xd;
(622 farmers in Gujarat State) with the specific aim of comparing&#xd;
one hypothesized ranking in terms of gross margin of (a) official&#xd;
Bt hybrids, (b) unofficial Bt hybrids, and (c) non-Bt hybrids.&#xd;
Results suggest that the official Bt varieties (MECH 12 and&#xd;
MECH 162) significantly outperform the unofficial varieties in&#xd;
terms of gross margin. However, unofficial, locally produced Bt&#xd;
hybrids can also perform significantly better than non-Bt&#xd;
hybrids, although second-generation (F2) Bt seed appears to&#xd;
have no yield advantage compared to non-Bt hybrids but can&#xd;
save on insecticide use. The paper explores some of the implications&#xd;
of this ranking.</dc:description>

<dc:description>We estimate changes in market structure in the US electric power industry during the last&#xd;
three decades using two independent approaches. First, we estimate an industry-wide&#xd;
conjectural-variations parameter. We find that industry concentration was generally rising during&#xd;
the 1970s, but has been gradually and consistently falling since the early 1980s. To check the&#xd;
robustness of these results, we then use a translog production function to estimate the Lerner&#xd;
Index. The results confirm that the industry was becoming less concentrated during the 1980s,&#xd;
though the Lerner Index tended to fluctuate during the late 1980s and early 1990s. Our results&#xd;
suggest that the current state of the electricity industry may have more to do with long-term&#xd;
changes in market structure than recent attempts to establish competitive wholesale electricity&#xd;
markets.</dc:description>

<dc:description>Though they developed in isolation, the theory of entrepreneurship and the&#xd;
economic theory of the firm can be usefully integrated. In particular, the concept of entrepreneurship&#xd;
as judgment associated with Knight (1921) and some Austrian school&#xd;
economists aligns naturally with the theory of the firm. Because judgment cannot be&#xd;
purchased on the market, the entrepreneur needs a firm — a set of alienable assets he&#xd;
controls — to carry out his function. We show how this notion of judgment illuminates&#xd;
key themes in the modern theory of the firm (existence, boundaries, and internal organization).&#xd;
In our approach, resource uses are not data, but are created as entrepreneurs&#xd;
envision new ways of using assets to produce final goods. The entrepreneur's problem&#xd;
is aggravated by the fact that capital assets are heterogeneous. Asset ownership allows&#xd;
the entrepreneur to experiment with novel combinations of heterogeneous assets. The&#xd;
boundaries of the firm, as well as aspects of internal organization, may also be understood&#xd;
as responses to entrepreneurial processes of experimentation.</dc:description>
